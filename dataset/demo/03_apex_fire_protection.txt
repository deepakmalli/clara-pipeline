Jordan: Hey Mike, good to connect. I know you've been talking with our team about automating your call handling. Let's start with some background on your business.

Jordan: Is that residential or commercial?

Mike: Primarily commercial. Warehouses, office buildings, retail spaces, restaurants. We do some multi-family residential — apartment complexes and condos — but nothing single-family. Our bread and butter is commercial fire sprinkler inspections and maintenance contracts.

Jordan: How big is the team?

Mike: Twelve field technicians, two project managers, and three office staff. We've got about 400 active maintenance contracts. The office gets probably 35 to 40 calls a day.

Jordan: Okay, that's solid volume. What's the main problem you're trying to solve?

Mike: Two things. One, we miss too many calls. My receptionist handles the front desk, incoming calls, and helps with permitting paperwork. When she's on a call, everything else goes to voicemail, and people don't leave voicemails anymore. They just call the next company. Two, we get a LOT of urgency-based calls, and I need someone — or something — that can tell the difference between "we need a fire watch because our sprinkler system is down and the fire marshal is here" versus "we want to schedule our annual inspection sometime next month."

Jordan: Those are very different levels of urgency. So let's define what an emergency looks like for Apex.

Mike: A fire sprinkler system that's been activated and is flooding a building — that's the big one. A system that's offline or impaired and the building needs a fire watch. Any call from a fire marshal or code enforcement. A broken main or riser. Or if a tenant or building manager reports fire sprinklers leaking or a zone down. Those all need immediate attention.

Jordan: And when someone calls with one of those?

Mike: Our emergency dispatch is handled by our senior project manager, Kevin Ross. His direct line is 770-555-0384. If Kevin doesn't answer within 60 seconds, try me directly at 770-555-0291. If neither of us picks up, take all their information — building name, address, the nature of the problem, and their phone number — and tell them someone will call back within 10 minutes. Fire emergencies don't wait.

Jordan: Absolutely. And for non-emergency calls?

Mike: For inspection scheduling, I want the AI to capture the building name, address, contact person, their phone and email, and what type of system they have if they know — wet, dry, pre-action, deluge. If they're an existing customer, try to pull up their maintenance contract. If they're new, get all the details and create a lead.

Jordan: What systems do you use internally?

Mike: We use HouseCall Pro for scheduling and dispatching. Our accounting is on QuickBooks. We keep a separate Excel tracker for inspection certifications, but I'd love to get away from that eventually.

Jordan: Good news — we integrate with HouseCall Pro. So new jobs can be created directly from calls. The QuickBooks piece would be separate.

And we don't do residential single-family fire sprinklers. If someone calls about any of those, just let them know politely that we specialize in commercial fire sprinkler systems.
